I started by building small internal tools. Over time, I started owning full features — from the backend to the UI to deployment. Recently, I built a customer-facing feature that made the page load 35% faster. Now I want a role where I get more ownership and work on bigger systems."

## SECTION 2 — HR: Behavioural (STAR Method) (Q16–Q40)

---

### Q16. Tell me about a time you missed a deadline.
> "I once thought a third-party API integration would take less time than it actually did. Midway through the sprint, I realised we would miss the deadline. I told my lead right away and suggested we ship a simple version first, then add extra filters later. We did that. Now I always spend some time researching unfamiliar integrations before I give an estimate."

---

### Q17. Conflict with a colleague — how did you resolve it?
> "A teammate and I disagreed on whether to use a stored procedure or LINQ for a report. Instead of arguing, I asked him why he preferred stored procedures. He had faced performance problems with LINQ before. We tested both approaches together, and the results settled the debate without any bad feeling."

---

### Q18. Time you had to learn quickly to deliver.
> "We needed real-time notifications using SignalR, and I had never used it before. I spent a weekend reading the docs and building a small test project. Then I paired with a teammate who had used WebSockets before. In one week, the feature was live, and I wrote a short guide for the team."

---

### Q19. When did you go beyond your job description?
> "New team members took almost a week to set up their local development environment. No one had time to fix this, so I spent a weekend writing a setup script and a short guide. Now new joiners get set up in under an hour. It wasn't my job, but it was an obvious problem to fix."

---

### Q20. Tell me about a project you're most proud of.
> "I rebuilt our order-tracking feature completely by myself — from the API to the Angular UI. I added caching for a slow endpoint and made the UI simpler. Support tickets about this feature dropped by more than half. It was the first project where I owned every part myself."

---

### Q21. When did you disagree with your manager?
> "My manager wanted to skip writing tests for a small feature to save time. I was worried since it touched billing logic. I showed him a real bug from the past that tests would have caught. He agreed to add basic test coverage. I learned to support my opinion with a real example, not just my gut feeling."

---

### Q22. Have you pushed back on requirements?
> "A product owner wanted a 'delete forever' button on user records with no confirmation step. I told her this was risky — someone could delete the wrong record by mistake. I suggested a soft delete with a recovery window instead. She agreed, and it has already saved us twice."

---

### Q23. Handling multiple priorities at once.
> "I once had a production bug, a sprint task, and an urgent request from another team, all in the same week. I ranked them by how much impact each had — bug first, sprint task second, other team's request third. I told everyone my plan so no one was surprised. Everything got done."

---

### Q24. Time you received critical feedback.
> "A senior developer told me my pull requests were too big to review properly. It felt a bit harsh at first, but he was right — I was mixing unrelated changes together. I started splitting my work into smaller PRs. My code got reviewed much faster after that."

---

### Q25. Tell me about a time you failed.
> "Early in my career, I pushed a database migration to production without testing it on data of a similar size. It locked a table for 20 minutes during work hours. I owned my mistake fully during the review meeting. Now I always test migrations on realistic data first."

---

### Q26. When did you convince your team to adopt a new practice?
> "I felt our team should write more unit tests, but people thought it would slow us down. Instead of arguing, I wrote good tests for one module myself. Those tests caught two bugs before they reached QA. After seeing that, the team started writing tests without me having to ask."

---

### Q27. Helping a struggling teammate.
> "A newer developer kept making small mistakes with async/await. I sat with him for 30 minutes every week for a month and walked through real examples from our code. Within two months, he was confidently reviewing async code in other people's pull requests."

---

### Q28. Delivering bad news to a stakeholder.
> "A payment gateway we used changed their API without much warning, and we couldn't hit our launch date. I told the product owner the same day I found out. I explained the problem and gave three options — delay, soft launch, or a temporary fix. She liked the early warning and we chose the temporary fix."

---

### Q29. Improving a process in your team.
> "Pull request reviews were taking 2–3 days, which blocked people a lot. I suggested a same-day review rule for small PRs and set up a Slack reminder. Review time dropped to a few hours within a month, and people stopped feeling stuck."

---

### Q30. Working under a manager you don't fully agree with.
> "My manager was very short and direct in his communication, which was different from what I was used to. I started writing short summaries after our talks to make sure I understood correctly. This reduced confusion, and we worked well together despite the style difference."

---

### Q31. Time you took ownership of something outside your scope.
> "Our staging environment kept breaking because of messy settings, and it wasn't really anyone's job to fix. I fixed it myself, made the settings consistent using environment variables, and wrote it down. It wasn't exciting work, but it saved the team a lot of time each week."

---

### Q32. A time you had to say no to a request.
> "A colleague from another team asked me to make a quick change directly in production, skipping our review process. I said no and explained the risk. I offered to fast-track it through a proper hotfix instead. It took 20 extra minutes but kept an untested change from going live."

---

### Q33. Handling ambiguous requirements.
> "I got a task to 'improve the search experience' without much detail. Instead of guessing, I met the product owner and asked what problem triggered this task. I gave three clear options. We picked one together, and we didn't waste time building the wrong thing."

---

### Q34. Time you had to work with a difficult stakeholder or client.
> "A client kept asking for changes in the middle of a sprint without knowing how it affected the timeline. I started sending a short weekly update showing what was in progress and what a new request would push out. This made the cost of changes clear, and requests became more thoughtful."

---

### Q35. A time you had to defend a technical decision.
> "I chose to use a message queue instead of direct API calls between two services. A teammate thought it added too much complexity. I explained that it protects us from losing data if a service goes down, and I gave an example of a real incident on another team that this design would have avoided. He agreed once he saw the real risk."

---

### Q36. Time you made a mistake in production.
> "I once deployed a wrong connection string that pointed to the wrong environment. Our monitoring alerts caught it within minutes. I rolled it back right away and told the team openly what happened. We added an extra review step for config changes after that."

---

### Q37. A time you collaborated across teams.
> "I worked with the mobile team to build a shared API for a new feature. Instead of building it alone and hoping it worked for them, I set up two short calls early to agree on the data format. This avoided a rework problem that had happened before on another project."

---

### Q38. How you handle repetitive or less exciting work.
> "I try to find small ways to make boring tasks easier — for example, I wrote a script to automate a manual data-cleanup task I had to do every sprint. When I can't automate something, I remind myself why it matters for the bigger picture, and that keeps me going."

---

### Q39. A time you had to give feedback to a peer.
> "A peer's pull requests often had no context, which made review slow. I mentioned it privately and showed him one of my own PRs as an example of the detail that helps. He started adding a short summary and testing notes, and his reviews got much faster."

---

### Q40. A time your priorities changed suddenly.
> "While building a new feature, a critical bug came up that was affecting live users. I paused the feature work, fixed the bug the same day, and then went back to the feature. I told my lead about the change right away so the sprint plan could be adjusted."

## SECTION 3 — Managerial Round: Ownership, Delivery & Teamwork (Q41–Q65)

---

### Q41. How do you approach a new task or feature end-to-end?
> "I first make sure I understand why we're building it, not just what to build. Then I break it into smaller parts — data model, API, UI, edge cases. I try to spot unknowns early, build step by step, and get feedback on the design early instead of only at the end. I leave time for testing before I call it done."

---

### Q42. How do you ensure code quality in your work?
> "I review my own code before I raise a pull request, write meaningful unit tests, and follow our team's coding standards. If I get the same review comment twice, I turn it into a personal checklist item so I stop repeating it."

---

### Q43. How do you handle unclear requirements?
> "I ask questions early instead of guessing and building the wrong thing. If the person giving the requirement isn't sure either, I suggest two or three clear options with pros and cons so we can decide together quickly."

---

### Q44. How do you estimate effort for a task?
> "I break the task into small pieces and look for anything unknown — if something is unclear, I spend a little time researching before giving an estimate. I base my estimate on similar past work, not on optimism. I also include time for testing and review, not just coding."

---

### Q45. How do you handle scope creep mid-sprint?
> "I don't just quietly take on extra work. I tell my lead or product owner about it clearly. Either something else moves out of the sprint, or the timeline changes. If I stay quiet, I usually end up missing a commitment later, which is worse."

---

### Q46. What do you do when you are blocked on something?
> "First, I try to solve it myself for a reasonable amount of time — checking docs, searching past discussions, trying different things. If I'm still stuck after that, I ask for help early instead of losing a whole day. Asking early is smart, not weak."

---

### Q47. How do you prioritize your daily tasks?
> "I first check if anything urgent came up overnight, like a production alert. Then I work on my sprint tasks in the order that helps others the most. I save deep-focus work for times when I have longer, uninterrupted blocks of time."

---

### Q48. How do you handle a tight deadline?
> "I first check if we can reduce the scope without losing the main value — just build the most important part first. I clearly tell everyone what's possible in the given time and what risks come with going faster. I'd rather be honest than promise something I can't deliver."

---

### Q49. Describe your ideal team.
> "A team where people can disagree openly without it becoming personal. Where code reviews are thorough but kind. And where people are happy to help each other when someone is stuck. I also like a team that admits mistakes openly, because it helps everyone improve."

---

### Q50. How do you handle disagreements on a technical approach with a teammate?
> "I try to focus on the idea, not the person — what are the pros and cons of each option? If we still can't agree, a quick test usually settles it with real data instead of just opinions. If it's still unclear, I'm fine trusting whoever has more experience in that area."

---

### Q51. How do you contribute to team knowledge sharing?
> "I write short notes when I solve something tricky, and I've done a few informal talks on things like async patterns and EF Core performance tips. I also explain my thinking clearly in pull request descriptions so the reviewer learns something too."

---

### Q52. How would you help onboard a new joiner if asked?
> "I'd explain the codebase and why key decisions were made, not just how things work. I'd give them a small, clear first task instead of something vague. I'd also check in with them daily during the first week, so small confusions don't turn into big problems."

---

### Q53. What's your approach to code reviews — giving and receiving?
> "When giving feedback, I focus on the code, not the person, and I explain why I'm suggesting a change instead of just saying I don't like it. When receiving feedback, I try not to get defensive — most comments actually make the code better, even the ones that feel a bit harsh at first."

---

### Q54. How do you balance technical debt with feature delivery?
> "I flag debt as soon as I see it instead of letting it pile up quietly. I fix small things when I'm already working in that part of the code. For bigger debt, I raise it with the team so it gets planned properly instead of being pushed aside forever."

---

### Q55. Describe how you'd handle a production issue outside work hours.
> "I'd first check how bad it really is — how many users, how serious? I'd share what I know right away, even if I don't have the full answer yet. I'd fix the immediate problem first, like a rollback, and do a proper root-cause check later once things are stable."

---

### Q56. How do you keep dependencies with other teams from becoming blockers?
> "I flag dependencies as early as possible, ideally during planning, instead of waiting until I actually need something. I check in regularly instead of just assuming it will be ready. If something looks at risk, I raise it early instead of waiting for it to become urgent."

---

### Q57. How do you handle being asked to deliver in half the estimated time?
> "I show the estimate honestly, task by task, so people understand why it takes that long. Then I talk about what's realistic — smaller scope, doing it in phases, or accepting some risk to move faster. I don't just say yes to something I don't believe we can actually do."

---

### Q58. What does "ownership" mean to you as a developer?
> "It means treating a feature as mine from start to finish, not just writing the code and forgetting about it. That includes thinking about edge cases, watching how it performs after release, and being the first person to respond if something goes wrong with it."

---

### Q59. How do you handle repetitive feedback on the same mistake?
> "I see it as a sign that I need a system, not just more effort. For example, if I keep forgetting null checks, I add it to a personal checklist before raising a pull request. Repeating the same mistake after being told once bothers me more than the mistake itself."

---

### Q60. Tell me about your experience mentoring or helping junior developers.
> "I've paired with junior developers on my team many times, walking them through how to debug instead of just fixing it for them. I try to ask guiding questions instead of giving direct answers, so they learn to figure it out themselves next time."

---

### Q61. How do you make sure your work aligns with business goals?
> "I try to understand why a feature is needed before I start building it — what problem it solves for the business. This helps me make better decisions on edge cases and priorities. If something doesn't seem connected to a clear goal, I ask instead of assuming."

---

### Q62. How do you handle context switching between multiple projects?
> "I try to group similar tasks together instead of jumping around constantly. I keep short notes on where I left off on each task so I don't waste time getting back into it. When switching is unavoidable, I'm clear about what's realistic on each project."

---

### Q63. What do you do when you don't agree with a technical decision made by the lead or architect?
> "I raise my concern clearly, with my reasoning, at the right time — usually during a design discussion, not after work has already started. If the decision still goes another way, I fully support it instead of complaining or working half-heartedly."

---

### Q64. How do you document your work?
> "I write short, practical notes focused on why a decision was made, since the code itself usually shows what it does. For anything tricky — like a workaround or an odd integration — I leave a comment or a short note so the next person, often me later, isn't confused."

---

### Q65. How would you handle a situation where a teammate isn't pulling their weight?
> "I'd first check in with them privately and kindly — there could be a reason I don't know about. If it keeps happening and affects the team, I'd mention it to my lead calmly, focusing on the impact, not blame, and let them handle it properly."

## SECTION 4 — Managerial Round: Communication & Stakeholder Handling (Q66–Q80)

---

### Q66. How do you communicate a delay to your manager or stakeholder?
> "I tell them as soon as I know, not at the deadline. I explain what caused the delay, what the new realistic timeline is, and what I need from them, if anything, to get back on track. Early and honest communication is always better than a last-minute surprise."

---

### Q67. How do you handle negative feedback from a client or stakeholder?
> "I listen fully without getting defensive, admit the problem, and ask questions to understand exactly what went wrong. Then I focus on a clear fix and a realistic timeline instead of making excuses."

---

### Q68. Describe a time you had to explain a technical issue to a non-technical person.
> "I had to explain to a product manager why a 'quick fix' would actually take a week. I compared it to fixing a house's foundation versus just repainting a wall. That helped her understand why something that looked simple was actually more complex."

---

### Q69. How do you manage up — communicating effectively with your manager?
> "I try to bring solutions along with problems, not just complaints. I give updates on my own instead of waiting to be asked, keep things short, and flag risks early before they become real problems."

---

### Q70. How do you handle vague or frequently changing requirements from a stakeholder?
> "I try to understand the real goal behind the request so I can build in a flexible way. When requirements change a lot, I make the cost of each change clear — what it pushes out — so decisions are made carefully instead of casually."

---

### Q71. How would you handle a stakeholder who keeps changing their mind mid-project?
> "I'd have an honest but respectful talk about how frequent changes affect the timeline and quality. I'd suggest locking the scope for short cycles, like a sprint, so changes are handled at fixed points instead of constantly mid-way."

---

### Q72. Tell me about a time you had to push back on a manager's decision.
> "My manager wanted to skip a staging deployment step before a release to save time. I explained the risk using a real past incident where skipping staging caused a bug in production. He agreed to keep the step, and we still met the deadline."

---

### Q73. How do you build trust with a new team or manager?
> "I listen and observe before sharing strong opinions, keep small promises consistently, and update people without waiting to be asked. Trust builds from being predictable, not from one big gesture."

---

### Q74. Describe your communication style.
> "I try to be direct but respectful — I'd rather raise a concern early than let it build up. I change how I talk based on who I'm talking to — more technical detail with developers, more simple, business-focused language with non-technical people."

---

### Q75. How do you handle a situation where your manager takes credit for your work?
> "I'd first assume it might not be on purpose and mention it to them calmly and directly — often it's just about visibility, not bad intent. If it kept happening, I'd start sharing my updates directly with wider stakeholders, like in demos or written summaries."

---

### Q76. How would you handle joining a team with an existing conflict?
> "I'd stay neutral at first and focus on the work instead of taking sides. If both sides shared their views naturally, I'd listen to both, but I wouldn't jump into an old conflict I wasn't part of. My goal would be building trust with everyone."

---

### Q77. Tell me about a time you received feedback you disagreed with.
> "A reviewer once said my code was over-engineered for a simple feature. I disagreed at first, but after thinking about it, I realized I had added flexibility for a future need that didn't even exist yet. I simplified the code. Sometimes disagreement just means I need time to see the other side."

---

### Q78. How do you handle working with someone whose communication style is very different from yours?
> "I try to adjust to them instead of expecting them to change. If they prefer written messages, I send a summary instead of just talking. If they're very direct, I match that instead of over-explaining. Meeting people where they are usually solves most of the friction."

---

### Q79. What would you do if you disagreed with the direction the team is taking on a project?
> "I'd raise my concern clearly, with reasoning, at the right time — ideally before too much work is already done. If the team still chooses a different direction, I'd fully support it instead of dragging my feet, since half-hearted support usually hurts delivery."

---

### Q80. How do you handle a situation with unclear ownership between team members?
> "I'd raise it openly instead of letting confusion cause duplicated or missed work. A quick conversation with whoever is coordinating the work usually fixes it fast. I'd rather over-communicate on ownership upfront than deal with confusion later."

### Q89. Do you have any questions for us?

### Q90. Anything in your profile that might concern us?

---

### Q91. What are you looking for in your next role?
> "I want real ownership of a product area, exposure to bigger, modern systems, and a team culture that values honest talk and continuous learning."

---

### Q92. How do you define success in your career?
> "Success to me means growing my skills regularly, having real ownership of meaningful work, and being someone my team can trust. Titles and pay matter, but they should come from doing good, trusted work — not the other way around."

---

### Q93. What would make you leave a company?
> "If I stop learning or feel there's no path forward — that would be a big reason. A poor culture fit, like dishonest communication or not respecting people's time, would also push me to leave. Pay matters too, but it's rarely the main reason for me."

---

### Q94. How do you handle a lower-than-expected offer?
> "I'd ask about the reasoning behind the number and share the market data and experience behind my expectation. I'm open to talking about the full structure — base, bonus, benefits — instead of just focusing on one number."

---

### Q95. Are you willing to relocate?
> "Yes, I'm open to relocating for the right opportunity, depending on details like timeline and any support the company offers for the move."

---

### Q96. What's your availability to join?
> "I'm currently serving a 60-day notice period, but I'm happy to talk about whether an earlier release is possible as things move forward."

---

### Q97. How do you handle counter-offers from your current company?
> "I'd listen honestly, but a counter-offer usually doesn't fix the real reason I started looking elsewhere, like limited growth or scope. More money alone doesn't usually change that for me."

---

### Q98. What's important to you besides salary?
> "Growth opportunities, the quality of the team I work with every day, and having real ownership over what I build. A good manager and a healthy work culture matter just as much as the salary number."

---

### Q99. How do you handle rejection (not getting selected)?
> "I ask for feedback if it's offered, take what's useful from it, and move on without dwelling on it too much. Not every role is the right fit, and I'd rather know that now than end up somewhere that doesn't suit me."

---

### Q100. Any final thoughts you'd like to share?
> "Just that I'm genuinely excited about this role and the chance to work with your team. I think my full stack background and how I approach ownership would fit well here, and I'm happy to answer any other questions you have."

## Quick Reference — Answer Frameworks

| Situation | Framework |
|-----------|-----------|
| Behavioural questions | STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) |
| Tough decisions / trade-offs | Options → Trade-offs → Recommendation |
| Salary negotiation | State expectation → back it up with data → show flexibility |
| Conflict resolution | Listen → Understand → Common ground → Outcome |
| Failure questions | Own it → Fix it → Change for the future |
| Giving feedback | SBI — Situation, Behaviour, Impact |
